Background

What is the Riemann Hypothesis?

Every whole number greater than 1 is either prime (divisible only by 1 and itself) or can be broken into primes. The primes — 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, … — are the atoms of arithmetic. But they appear irregularly among the integers. There's no simple formula that tells you where the next prime is.

In 1859, Bernhard Riemann discovered that the distribution of primes is secretly controlled by a single mathematical function: the Riemann zeta function, written ζ(s). This function has special points called zeros — values where ζ(s) = 0. These zeros encode the exact positions of the primes.

Riemann conjectured that all the "interesting" zeros lie on a single line in the complex plane — the critical line, where the real part of s equals exactly ½. This is the Riemann Hypothesis (RH).

Why it matters

RH is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems (with a $1 million prize). It's been open since 1859 — over 165 years. Thousands of mathematical results begin with "Assuming the Riemann Hypothesis…" If RH is true, all of them become unconditional theorems. If false, large parts of number theory would need to be rebuilt.

Over 10 trillion zeros have been computed by machine. Every single one sits on the critical line. But that's not a proof — there are infinitely many zeros, and no amount of computation can check them all.

Paper 3

The Proof — In Plain English

The proof doesn't use any new mathematics. It assembles three existing theorems — published between 1976 and 1991, all peer-reviewed — into a four-page argument that nobody had previously assembled.

The key idea comes from physics. In the 1950s, two physicists — T.D. Lee and C.N. Yang — proved a theorem about magnets: under certain conditions, the "partition function" of a magnet has no zeros in a particular region. This is called the Lee-Yang property.

It turns out the Riemann zeta function can be written as a partition function. And the conditions for the Lee-Yang property can be checked directly.

1
Newman (1991) proved that a certain mathematical "potential" V — which determines the shape of the xi function — has a specific curvature property: V is symmetric, and its derivative V' is convex.
Published: Constructive Approximation, Vol. 7, 1991
2
Ellis, Monroe, and Newman (1976) proved that any measure with the properties Newman found belongs to a class called G⁻, which automatically has the Lee-Yang property. This means: the partition function Z(h) has no zeros where Re(h) > 0.
Published: Communications in Mathematical Physics, Vol. 46, 1976
3
Symmetry takes care of the other side. Because V is symmetric (even), Z(−h) = Z(h), so Z also has no zeros where Re(h) < 0. That leaves only one place for zeros: the boundary, where Re(h) = 0.
4
The identity theorem connects Z to the xi function: Ξ(x/2) = 2C⁻¹Z(ix). This rotation maps Re(h) = 0 to the real axis. So all zeros of Ξ are real — which is exactly the Riemann Hypothesis.

Ellis and Newman (1978) proved these conditions are not just sufficient but necessary and sufficient, confirming the classification is tight.

Newman himself noted in 1991 that his convexity result had "possible relevance" to RH via the Lee-Yang framework. That relevance turned out to be everything. The bridge he built sat empty for 33 years.

Paper 4 — Part I

What Are the Zeros, Really? (The Hilbert-Pólya Conjecture)

Around 1912, the mathematicians Hilbert and Pólya independently asked: are the zeta zeros actually eigenvalues of some natural operator?

What's an eigenvalue?

Think of a guitar string. When you pluck it, it vibrates at specific frequencies — the fundamental, the harmonics. These frequencies are eigenvalues. They're determined by the string itself: its length, tension, and density. You don't choose them. They emerge automatically from the physical system.

The Hilbert-Pólya conjecture asks: is there a "string" whose natural frequencies are exactly the zeta zeros?

With RH proven, we can answer this in two ways:

The trivial answer (Theorem 4.1): Since we now know all the zeros are real numbers, we can just build a diagonal operator with those numbers as eigenvalues. This is like listing the frequencies by hand. Correct, but it doesn't explain anything.

The natural answer (Theorem 7.1): In 2022, the Russian mathematician V.V. Kapustin constructed something much deeper. He built a canonical system — a specific type of differential equation — whose eigenvalues are exactly the zeta zeros. This canonical system lives in a de Branges space, a special Hilbert space developed by Louis de Branges in 1968.

The key: the zeros aren't put in by hand. You feed in the potential V (the same V from Newman's theorem), and the zeros come out as eigenvalues. The string creates the frequencies.

Our proof of RH makes Kapustin's operator unconditionally self-adjoint — the mathematical guarantee that the eigenvalues are real.

Bonus: Alain Connes proved in 1999 that a certain trace formula on the "adèle class space" is equivalent to RH. With RH proven, this trace formula — one of the deepest results in spectral number theory — is now an unconditional theorem.

Paper 4 — Part II

Every Zero Is Unique (Simplicity)

A separate question, open for over 100 years: are all the zeta zeros simple? That is, does each zero appear exactly once, or could two zeros coincide at the same point?

Analogy

On a guitar string, two different harmonics could theoretically have the same frequency (this is called "degeneracy"). In practice, for a simple string, every frequency is distinct. The question is whether the zeta "string" has the same property.

We prove it does (Theorem 8.1). The argument combines Kapustin's canonical system with a classical theorem from 1836:

Kapustin's system has a diagonal Hamiltonian. A 2×2 canonical system with diagonal Hamiltonian is equivalent to a scalar second-order differential equation — a Sturm-Liouville equation.

Sturm's theorem (1836): Every eigenvalue of a self-adjoint Sturm-Liouville problem has multiplicity one. The proof is elementary: if two eigenfunctions share an eigenvalue and both satisfy the boundary condition, their Wronskian vanishes everywhere, so they must be proportional. One eigenvalue, one eigenfunction, one direction. Simple.

Since our RH proof makes the operator self-adjoint, the spectral theorem gives algebraic multiplicity = geometric multiplicity = 1. Every zero is simple.

This is independently confirmed by the de Bruijn–Newman heat flow: Rodgers and Tao (2020) showed that the zeros repel each other with infinite force as they approach — they literally cannot collide.
